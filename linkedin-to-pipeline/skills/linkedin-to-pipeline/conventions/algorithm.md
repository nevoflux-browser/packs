# Algorithm — reach is earned by performance, not follower count

Reach on LinkedIn is **not** tied to your audience size. It's tied to performance.

The distribution model works in one loop:

1. Every post is shown to a **small group first**.
2. The system **measures the response**.
3. If the signals are strong, distribution **expands**; if weak, it **slows**.

This is why a post can outperform your follower count — and why chasing one viral spike is
the wrong goal. **Growth comes from repeated performance**, not a single hit.

## The signals that matter most

- **Early engagement** — comments/reactions in the first window, from that first small group.
- **Dwell time** — how long people actually stay on the post.
- **Relevance** — how well it matches the audience it's shown to.

## What this implies for the rest of the system

- The **hook and visual** decide early engagement and dwell time (see `hooks.md`), so they
  get the most effort.
- **Consistency beats spikes** — a steady 2–3 posts a week of repeatable quality outperforms
  an occasional viral attempt. This is why the calendar is built around a rolling window, not
  a one-time push (see `calendar-arc.md`).
- Timing a post so its **first audience is active** helps early engagement; use this only as a
  soft heuristic when suggesting a publish slot, never as a hard rule.
